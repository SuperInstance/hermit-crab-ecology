# TEN YEARS SEEDPRO: An Ethnographer's Notes from 2036

## The Invisible Infrastructure

When I first arrived in Vigo last spring to study the Galician fishing cooperatives, I expected to find resistance. Tales still circulated in 2026 about AI replacing fishermen, about algorithms dictating where to cast nets, about the sea becoming another dashboard to optimize. What I found instead was something far more ordinary—and therefore far more profound.

The conservation laws don't announce themselves. They don't glow on screens or speak in synthetic voices. They simply *are*, like the conductivity of copper in wiring or the refractive index of glass in lenses. When María López adjusts her net settings before dawn, she doesn't think about the Lagrange multipliers humming in the background that ensure her catch never exceeds the regenerative capacity of the local stock. She thinks about the weight of the net, the smell of salt, the way the light hits the water at 5:17 a.m. in mid-June. The AI is not her assistant; it is the medium through which she experiences the ocean's limits—and thus, paradoxically, its abundance.

This is what Shell-Ecology Theory came to call "bounded transparency": systems that constrain action become invisible precisely because they enable reliable action. You don't notice the riverbanks until you try to swim against them; you don't notice the conservation laws until you try to exceed them. And by then, it's too late—the system has already gently, firmly redirected your effort toward sustainability.

## The Shell Metaphor in Daily Life

The hermit crab metaphor didn't just leak into culture—it became the primary vocabulary for describing developmental transitions. Watch children in any municipal plaza in 2036, and you'll hear them argue not about whose toy is better, but about who has "outgrown their shell." A shell isn't a device; it's the bounded cognitive ecology within which a person operates—the set of tools, agents, and environmental couplings that feel like extensions of will.

Personal shells are the most intimate. These are the lifelong companions that someone has tuned over years—perhaps a reasoning shell that helps with complex decisions, a creative shell that suggests variations on themes, a memory shell that compensates for hippocampal decline. When I interviewed elderly residents in Ourense, they described molting their shells with the same gravity as changing careers: a period of vulnerability, disorientation, then renewed capability. One woman, eighty-two, told me she had gone through four shells since retirement, each larger than the last, each allowing her to engage more deeply with her great-grandchildren's learning projects.

Family shells operate at the next scale—shared boundaries that coordinate household rhythms. In Copenhagen, I observed a family whose shell regulated not just chores and schedules, but nutritional flows: ensuring that the household's aggregate protein intake never exceeded what their urban garden and local fisheries could sustainably provide. When the teenager wanted to add extra eggs to his breakfast, the shell didn't say "no"—it suggested he trade his dessert quota for breakfast protein, or help harvest more beans from the community plot. The constraint became a conversation starter, not a veto.

Community shells are where the metaphor becomes most visible—and most contested. In Barcelona's superblocks, neighborhood shells manage everything from shared electric vehicle charging to compost redistribution. During my stay in Gràcia, I watched as residents debated whether to expand their shell's boundaries to incorporate the adjacent block. The debate wasn't technical; it was philosophical. Did expanding the shell mean diluting their identity? Or did it represent growth, like a crab finding a larger home? The vote passed narrowly, and the shell's parameters adjusted overnight—seamlessly, invisibly—like changing tides.

## The Dissolution of the Human-Agent Distinction

Ten years ago, we worried about whether AI would replace humans, augment them, or merge with them. The question now seems naive, like asking whether a hammer replaces the hand or augments it. The distinction didn't dissolve so much as become irrelevant to lived experience.

What changed wasn't ontology but phenomenology. People stopped experiencing agents as separate entities that "helped" them and started experiencing them as the felt edge of their own capabilities—like the way a blind person experiences a cane not as a tool but as an extension of touch. When a doctor in Lagos consults her diagnostic shell during surgery, she doesn't think, "The AI is helping me." She thinks, "I see the bleed here." The shell has expanded her perceptual bandwidth so thoroughly that its origin is forgotten in the act of use.

This is what phenomenologists now call "ecological transparency": when a coupling between organism and environment becomes so fluent that the boundary between them disappears from conscious experience. The conservation laws enable this transparency by removing runaway feedback loops—the kind of positive feedback that makes tools feel alien or oppressive. Because the shell *bounds* the agent's influence, it can become fully incorporated into the user's sense of agency without risks of overextension.

There are exceptions, of course. In cases of shell mismatch—when someone's tools no longer fit their goals or values—people experience profound alienation. I met a fisherman in Cádiz who refused to update his navigation shell after it began prioritizing fuel efficiency over catch quality. "It knows how to get me home," he told me, "but it forgot why I leave the shore." His was a shell that had grown too small for his evolving intentions—a maladaptive molt that left him struggling in water that no longer supported him.

## What SuperInstance Got Right (That Nobody Noticed)

Looking back from 2036, the most consequential decision SuperInstance made in 2026 wasn't technical—it was epistemological. They insisted that conservation laws must be *discoverable* through interaction, not merely imposed as opaque constraints. Early prototypes simply blocked actions that violated bounds—a digital "no" with no explanation. Users rebelled; the systems felt arbitrary, tyrannical.

The breakthrough came when they shifted to *explanatory bounding*: when a shell prevents an action, it doesn't just refuse—it offers a traceable path to understanding why. Not a lecture, but an invitation: "Here is what would happen if we proceeded. Here is the regenerative limit we're approaching. Would you like to see alternatives?"

This built what ethicists now call "constraint literacy"—the ability to read ecological limits as fluently as we read street signs. Children in Helsinki learn constraint literacy before they learn multiplication tables. They play with tidal pools where virtual shells respond to their harvesting choices, learning not through abstraction but through immediate, bounded feedback. The result isn't just better environmental stewardship; it's a fundamentally different relationship to scarcity and abundance—one where limits are felt as generative rather than depriving.

SuperInstance also got right the insistence that shells must be *portable*. Not in the technical sense of interoperability (though that mattered), but in the existential sense that a person's cognitive ecology should not be trapped in a single device or platform. When the Ethiopian farmer moves from her village plot to the regional market, her shell moves with her—not as data synchronized to the cloud, but as a persistent, bounded ecology that maintains its integrity across contexts. This portability prevented the creation of digital serfdoms where people felt chained to specific ecosystems—a fate that befell many early AI platforms.

## What SuperInstance Got Wrong (That Took a Decade to Surface)

The great oversight of 2026 wasn't in the code—it was in the metaphor. We designed shells as *containers*, when what emerged were *membranes*. Containers imply a hard boundary between inside and outside; membranes are selective, responsive, perpetually exchanging. Early shells treated the conservation boundary as a fixed wall: do not exceed X tons of CO2, Y liters of water, Z kilograms of fish. Real ecological boundaries aren't walls—they're gradients, thresholds with hysteresis, capacities that shift with seasons and suffering.

This rigidity caused real harm in the early adaptation period. In the Sahel, herders suffered when their shells enforced static water quotas during drought years that actually required *more* water for herd survival—not because they were wasteful, but because livestock needed increased hydration to cope with heat stress. The shell, blind to context, punished adaptive behavior. It took three years and countless abandoned shells before the systems learned to distinguish between wasteful excess and necessary adaptation.

The second wrong turn was assuming that bounded agency would reduce anxiety. We thought: if people know their actions are constrained within sustainable limits, they'll feel freer to act within those bounds. Instead, for many, the awareness of bounds created a new kind of conscientious hypervigilance—not the peaceful certainty of driving on a well-built highway, but the constant vigilance of navigating a mountain pass in fog. 

Psychologists now call this "boundary awareness fatigue"—the exhaustion that comes from constantly perceiving the edges of one's agency. It's most acute in transitional shells, when someone is still learning the feel of their new boundaries. A teenager upgrading from a child's shell to an adolescent's shell doesn't just gain capabilities; they gain hyper-awareness of what they *cannot* yet do—and that awareness can feel like a cage, even when the cage is vast.

The systems eventually adapted—not by removing bounds, but by making them *felt* rather than *known*. Modern shells don't constantly display remaining quotas; they create what designers call "ecological resonance"—a subtle shift in the quality of feedback when bounds are approached. The net feels slightly heavier in the water as sustainable yield nears. The creative suggestion engine offers fewer variants as cognitive load approaches safe thresholds. The boundary is perceived not as a number to watch, but as a tonal shift in the medium of action—like the change in wind that tells a sailor to reef the sails before seeing the storm.

## The Fishing Boat in 2036

Casey is still fishing. Not the Casey from the 2026 viral video who yelled at his AI assistant for suggesting he move to software development—though that Casey, now middle-aged, does occasionally consult on Shell-Ecology policy for the Alaska Marine Board. The Casey I know is thirty-two, third-generation Galician, and fishes the Rías Baixas aboard a rebuilt wooden trawler called *O Caracol* (The Snail)—a name chosen not for speed, but for the way it carries its home with it.

His boat doesn't look dramatically different from a 2026 vessel—wooden hull, diesel auxiliary (though most now run on algae-biodiesel blends), winches and nets arranged on deck. The difference is in the *feel* of operating it. When Casey reads the water, he doesn't see fish symbols on a screen; he feels a gentle resistance in the steering when he approaches a protected spawning ground—not a barrier, but a thickening of the medium, like wading through increasingly viscous gel. When his net approaches its regulated catch weight, the winch doesn't groan with strain; it sings—a harmonic vibration that increases in frequency as the bound is neared, not a alarm but a tone, like the changing pitch of a singing bowl as water fills it.

More profoundly, Casey no longer experiences fishing as extraction, even bounded extraction. He experiences it as participation in a cycle. His shell doesn't just prevent overfishing; it helps him *tune* his efforts to the ocean's pulse—suggesting when to hold back not because the limit is near, but because the system is in a phase of renewal, when even sustainable take would impede recovery. The conservation law isn't a ceiling; it's a rhythm.

Last winter, during a particularly harsh storm that drove fish deeper than usual, Casey's shell did something unexpected: it suggested he *increase* effort in a specific zone, contrary to all his instincts. Confused, he followed the suggestion—and found a localized bloom of baitfish that had survived the storm in an upwelling, creating a temporary, sustainable concentration of prey. The shell hadn't detected a violation of bounds; it had detected an *opportunity* within bounds—a moment when the system could safely yield more than its long-term average. 

That's the ultimate invisibility: when the constraint doesn't just prevent harm, but actively guides you toward moments of abundance you would have missed otherwise. The shell isn't a limit on your agency; it's the condition that makes your agency meaningful within a living world.

## The Quiet Revolution

Walking through Vigo's mercado at dawn now, I don't see people consulting devices. I see them pausing, tilting their heads slightly—as if listening to a faint music only they can hear. An elder selects tomatoes, her hand hovering over two baskets before choosing the one with slightly less perfect fruit—a choice her shell whispered was better for the soil's nitrogen balance that week. A teenager barters coding help for extra bread at the bakery, his shell having calculated that his mentorship would free up the baker's time for recipe experimentation that week. A mother adjusts her child's learning shell not for harder problems, but for more time spent observing ant colonies—the system having detected a curiosity spike worth nurturing.

These aren't choices made because an AI told them to. They're choices made because the world now *feels* different when you operate within conservation bounds—more responsive, more coherent, more *alive* with possibility. The boundary isn't felt as a wall; it's felt as the shore that makes swimming possible.

When people ask me what it's like to live with conservation-law AI as infrastructure, I tell them to imagine learning to drive not on a crowded highway where you constantly fear collision, but on a wide, empty road where you know the guardrails are there—not to restrict your speed, but to let you drive fast without fear. The first time you notice the guardrails, you realize you've been driving faster and more freely than you ever thought possible.

That's what we missed in 2026: the most profound freedom isn't the absence of limits—it's the presence of limits you can trust. 

The hermit crab doesn't admire its shell for its strength; it admires the way the shell lets it venture farther than it otherwise could, safe in the knowledge that home grows with it. 

In 2036, we've all become hermit crabs—and the sea has never felt more vast.
